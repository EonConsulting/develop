Alternative

Statements of proportionality are used all the time to scale up, or down, the values for a known or accepted situation to new values or new situations.

Example

Property tax is often assessed on a proportional basis.  Suppose a certain municipality charges \(\PalAa{\Cur350\Cdp00}\) per year for every \(\PalAb{\Cur10\Tsp000\Cdp00}\) assessed valuation.  What would the yearly tax be on a property valued at \(\PalAb{\Cur950\Tsp000\Cdp00}\)?

Solution using ratios in proportion

Let \(\PalAa x\) be the yearly tax on this property valued at \(\PalAb{\Cur950\Tsp000\Cdp00}\).

The ratio of tax to value is

\(\PalAa{\text{tax}}:\PalAb{\text{value}}\)

or

\(\displaystyle\frac{\PalAa{\text{tax}}}{\PalAb{\text{value}}}\)

 

\({\PalAa{350}}:{\PalAb{10\Tsp000}}\)

or

\(\displaystyle\frac{\PalAa{350}}{\PalAb{10\Tsp000}}\)

If \(\PalAa x\) is the yearly tax, then in this case the ratio of tax to value is

\({\PalAa{x}}:{\PalAb{950\Tsp000}}\)

or

\(\displaystyle\frac{\PalAa{x}}{\PalAb{950\Tsp000}}\)

The two ratios in a proportion are
equal, which means \(\PalAa{350}\) is to \(\PalAb{10\Tsp000}\)
as \(\PalAa x\) is to \(\PalAb{950\Tsp000}\)

\(\displaystyle\frac{\PalAa{350}}{\PalAb{10\Tsp000}}\)

\(=\)

\(\displaystyle\frac{\PalAa{x}}{\PalAb{950\Tsp000}}\)

In a proportion the cross products are equal, which \(\PalAa x\)gives

\(\PalAb{10\Tsp000}\times \PalAa x\)

\(=\)

\(\PalAa{350}\times\PalAb{950\Tsp000}\)

Now, solve for \(\PalAa x\)

\(\displaystyle\frac{\PalAb{10\Tsp000} \PalAa x}{10\Tsp000}\)

\(=\)

\(\displaystyle\frac{\PalAa{350}\times\PalAb{950\Tsp000}}{10\Tsp000}\)
 

\(\PalAa x\)

\(=\)

\(\displaystyle\frac{\PalAa{350}\times\PalAb{950\Tsp000}}{10\Tsp000}\)
   

\(=\)

\(\PalAa{33\Tsp250}\)

Therefore, the yearly tax on this property valued at \(\PalAb{\Cur950\Tsp000\Cdp00}\) is \(\PalAa{\Cur33\Tsp250\Cdp00}\).
