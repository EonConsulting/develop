Farmer example (Cont.)

 A peanut farmer’s cost function in rand, is given by

\(\PalAa {C(\PalAb x)=\displaystyle\frac{\PalAb x^2}{100}+100}\)

where \(\PalAb x\) represents the number of kilograms of peanuts produced.

The slope of this cost function is not constant and there is no constant rate of change. The slope changes continuously.  

The farmer wants to know if he produces \({1\Tsp000}\) kilograms of peanuts, how will the cost change if one extra kilogram is produced.  This means, he wants to determine the marginal cost at a production level of \(\PalAb{1\Tsp000}\)
kilograms.

He uses the following procedure:

The cost function is

\(\PalAa{ C(\PalAb x)}\)

\(=\)

\(\displaystyle\frac{\PalAb x^2}{100}+100\text{.}\)

Determine the derivative of the first term: 

\(\displaystyle\frac{d}{dx}\left(\displaystyle\frac{\PalAb x^2}{100} \right)\)

\(=\)

\(\displaystyle\frac{2\PalAb x^{2-1}}{100}\)
   

\(=\)

\(\displaystyle\frac{\PalAb x}{50}\)

Determine the derivative of the second term: 

\(\displaystyle\frac{d}{dx}\left(100 \right)\)

\(=\)

\(0\)

So, if the cost function is 

\(\PalAa{ C(\PalAb x)}\)

\(=\)

\(\displaystyle\frac{\PalAb x^2}{100}+100\text{,}\)

then the derivative of the cost function is

\(\PalBb {C'(\PalAb x)}\)

\(=\)

\(\displaystyle\frac{\PalAb x}{50}\text{.}\)

Substitute

\(\PalAb x\)

\(=\)

\(\PalAb{1\Tsp000}\)

into the derivative

\(\PalBb {C'(\PalAb x)}\)

\(=\)

\(\displaystyle\frac{\PalAb x}{50}\)

to calculate the marginal cost at a production level of \(\PalAb{1\Tsp000}\) kilograms:

\(\PalBb {C'(\PalAb {1\Tsp000})}\)

\(=\)

\(\displaystyle\frac{\PalAb {1\Tsp000}}{50}\)
   

\(=\)

\(\PalBb{20}\text{.}\)

This means, for an extra kilogram produced at a production level of \(\PalAb{1\Tsp000}\) kilograms, the cost will increase by \(\PalBb{\Cur20\Cdp00}\).
