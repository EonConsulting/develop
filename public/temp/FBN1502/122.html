Interest compounded semi-annually

Suppose Paul invests \(\Cur600\Cdp00\) for three years at a compounded interest rate of \(10\%\) per annum, but the interest is compounded semi-annually. This means his interest is added to the principal every six months, not every year.

Principal after first half-year

Calculate the amount of interest he has earned and his new principal after the first six months, or half-year.

We can calculate the amount of interest he has earned and his new principal after the first six months in the following way:

Interest formula

\(I\)

\(=P\times i \times n\)

Interest earned after first six months

\(I\)

\(=\Cur600\Cdp00\times 0\Ndp10\times\PalAb{0\Ndp5}=\Cur30\Cdp00\)

New principal

\(=P+I\)

New principal after first six months

\(=\Cur600\Cdp00+\Cur30\Cdp00=\Cur630\Cdp00\)

So after the first six months, or half-year, the amount of interest he has earned is \(\Cur30\Cdp00\) and his new principal is \(\Cur630\Cdp00\).

We can also calculate his new principal after the first six months in the following way:

For a half-year period his interest percentage is cut in half from \(10\%\) to \(5\%\), so that the interest he earns is \(5\%\) of \(\Cur600\Cdp00\). Thus his new principal after six months is \(105\%\) of \(\Cur600\Cdp00\).

Half-year period interest \(\%\)

\(\displaystyle\frac{1}{\PalAb2}\text{year}\:I=I\div\PalAb2=10\%\div2=5\%\)

Interest earned after first six months

\(=\displaystyle\frac{1}{2}\text{year}\:I\times P=5\%\times \Cur600\Cdp00=\Cur30\Cdp00\)

New principal after first six months

\(=\left(\displaystyle\frac{1}{2}\:\text{year}\:I+100\%\right)\times P\)

 

\(=(5\%+100\%)\times \Cur600\Cdp00=105\%\times \Cur600\Cdp00\)

 

\(=1\Ndp05\times \Cur600\Cdp00=\Cur630\Cdp00\)

So, his new principal after the first six months is \(\Cur630\Cdp00\).

Principal after second half year

His new principal after another six months, that is after one year, is \(105\%\) of \(\Cur630\Cdp00\). It is calculated in the following way:

New principal after second six months

\(=\left(\displaystyle\frac{1}{2}\:\text{year}\:I+100\%\right)\times (P\:\text{after first six months})\)

 

\(=(5\%+100\%)\times \Cur630\Cdp00=105\%\times \Cur630\Cdp00\)

 

\(=1\Ndp05\times \Cur630\Cdp00=\Cur661\Cdp50\)

So, his new principal after another six months, that is after one year, is \(\Cur661\Cdp50\).

Principal after one year

The same reasoning shows that Paul's principal gets multiplied by the factor \(1\Ndp05\) every half-year. After one year, two half-year periods have passed, so Paul's money has been multiplied by \(1\Ndp05\) twice. His principal amount after one year is calculated in the following way:

New principal after one year

\(=P\times\left(\displaystyle\frac{1}{2}\:\text{year}\:I+100\%\right)\times\left(\displaystyle\frac{1}{2}\:\text{year}\:I+100\%\right) \)

 

\(=\Cur600\Cdp00\times105\%\times105\%=\Cur600\Cdp00\times1\Ndp05\times1\Ndp05\)
 

\(=\Cur600\Cdp00\times1\Ndp05^2=\Cur600\Cdp00\times1\Ndp1025=\Cur661\Cdp50\)

So, his principal amount after one year is the same as when calculated after two half-year time periods, namely \(\Cur661\Cdp50\).

Principal after three years

After three years, six half-year time periods have passed. So, after three years we can calculate Paul's principal in the same way:

New principal after three years

\(=P\times\left(\displaystyle\frac{1}{2}\:\text{year}\:I+100\%\right)^6 \)
 

\(=\Cur600\Cdp00\times(105\%)^6=\Cur600\Cdp00\times1\Ndp05^6\)

 

\(=\Cur600\Cdp00\times1\Ndp340095=\Cur804\Cdp06\)

Periodic interest rate

This interest rate of \(5\%\) is called the periodic interest rate for Paul's loan.  It represents the percentage of the principal his money earns over the time period between compoundings. 

For interest compounded semi-annually, the periodic
interest rate is half the annual interest rate.
