Solving linear equations by using \(x\)-intercepts

Linear equations can be solved with a step-by-step method, however there is no reason why short-cuts and and adding several terms at once cannot be used.  However, if you are unsure about using alternative methods, rather be on the safe side and work step by step.  

A linear equation can be solved by determining the \(x\)-intercept of the corresponding line.  This is another method of solving linear equations in one variable.  Any linear equation \(y=ax+b\) can be solved by using this method:

Step 1

Manipulate the linear equation, using operations, until it is in the form:

\(ax+b=0\)

The left-hand side is the general equation for a linear function.

Step 2

This equation is solved by adding \(-b\) to both sides to get:

\(\begin{align}ax+b-b&=-b\\ ax&=-b \end{align}\)

Step 3

Divide by \(a\), assuming \(a\neq0\), to obtain the root or solution:

\(x={\displaystyle-\frac{b}{a}}\)

The last equation in this method is the equation for the intercept on the \(x\)-axis of the linear function \(y=ax+b\).  This intercept occurs at \(y=0\).

In other words, we can always interpret the root of a linear equation in one variable as the \(x\)-intercept of the corresponding linear function as shown below:

The equation of the line in this graph is \(y=ax+b\).  At the \(x\)-intercept point \(y=0\), therefore \(ax+b=0\) and the value of \(x\) is \({-\displaystyle\frac{b}{a}}\).
