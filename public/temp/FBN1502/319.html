How it works

Jane wants to borrow \(\Cur5\Tsp000\Cdp00\) from the bank for a period of two years at a simple interest rate of \(\PalAa{12\%}\) per year.  Jane pays \(12\%\) for the first year and then another \(12\%\) for the second year.  

This is what happens:

  Today Jane borrows \(\Cur5\Tsp000\Cdp00\) from the bank.
  Next year Jane owes the bank \(\Cur600\Cdp00 \) interest.
  After two years Jane pays back \(\Cur5\Tsp000\Cdp00\) plus \(\Cur1\Tsp200\Cdp00\) interest.
  \(\Cur600\Cdp00 \) interest for the first year and \(\Cur600\Cdp00 \) for the second year.
  So, Jane borrows \(\Cur5\Tsp000\Cdp00\), but has to pay back \(\Cur6\Tsp200\Cdp00\).

The interest Jane pays for two years is:

\(\Cur5\Tsp000\Cdp00\times\PalAa{0\Ndp12}\times\PalAb 2 =\Cur1\Tsp200\Cdp00 \)

The interest is \(\Cur1\Tsp200\Cdp00\).

That is how simple interest works, you pay the same amount of interest every year.  In this case it was \(\Cur600\Cdp00 \) for the first year and \(\Cur600\Cdp00 \) for the second year.
