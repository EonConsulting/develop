Linear relationship between total cost and quantity

You can calculate marginal cost when you have a linear relationship between total cost and quantity.

Consider the example of a factory that manufactures office desks.  The manufacturing costs for a month are:

  cost of wood and other materials amount to \(\Cur105\Cdp00\) per desk,
  direct labour costs \(\Cur175\Cdp00\) per desk,
  variable factory overheads amount to \(\Cur70\Cdp00\) per desk and
  fixed factory overheads amount to \(\Cur100\Tsp000\Cdp00\) for the month.

The total monthly cost of the factory is calculated as:

\(\begin{align} \PalAa{TC}&=\PalBc{\text{fixed cost}}+\PalAb{\text{variable cost}}\\ &={100\Tsp000}+350\PalAb{q}. \end{align} \)

The total cost is a linear function of the number of desks \((\PalAb q)\) manufactured.

Marginal cost is the additional cost we have when we manufactured one more chair. With the equation \(\PalAa{TC}=100\Tsp000+350\PalAb q\), our total cost goes up by \(\Cur350\Cdp00\) whenever we manufacture an additional desk, as shown by the coefficient in front of the \(\PalAb q\).

So we have a constant marginal cost of \(\Cur350\Cdp00\) per desk produced.

Let us consider linear functions.  The equation for a linear function, or straight line, is \(\PalAa y=a\PalAb x+b\) where \(a\) is the slope of the line and \(b\) is the intercept of the line on the \(y\)-axis.

In terms of two arbitrary points on the straight line, \(P_1\) with co-ordinates \((\PalAb{x_1};\PalAa{y_1})\) and \(P_2\) with co-ordinates \((\PalAb{x_2};\PalAa{y_2})\), the slope is defined as:

 

\(\begin{align} a &= \displaystyle\frac{\PalAa{\text{change in } y}}{\PalAb{\text{change in } x}} \\ &= \displaystyle\frac{\PalAa{y_2-y_1}}{\PalAb{x_2-x_1}} \end{align}\)
