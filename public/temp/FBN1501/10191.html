Introduction 1

A decimal number, based on the number \(10\), contains a decimal comma. 
The word 'decimal' really means 'based on \(10\)' (From the Latin word decima meaning 'a tenth part').

Place value

To understand decimal numbers you must first know about place value.
When we write numbers, the position or placement of each digit is important.

In the number \(634\) the \(4\) is in the units position, meaning just \(4\) or \(4\) ones; the \(3\) is in the tens position, meaning \(3\) tens or thirty; and the \(6\) is in the hundreds position, meaning \(6\) hundreds or six hundred.  

This can be illustrated with the number \(634\) as follows:

    \(6\)   \(3\)   \(4\)    
    Hundreds \(\PalAa\leftarrow\) Tens \(\PalAa\leftarrow\) Units    
    \(100\text{s}\) \(\PalAa\leftarrow\) \(10\text{s}\) \(\PalAa\leftarrow\) \(1\text{s}\)    
    \(\PalAa{(10\times\text{ bigger})}\)   \(\PalAa{(10\times\text{ bigger})}\)        
                 
    \(6\)   \(3\)   \(4\)    
    Hundreds \(\PalAb\rightarrow\) Tens \(\PalAb\rightarrow\) Units    
    \(100\text{s}\) \(\PalAb\rightarrow\) \(10\text{s}\) \(\PalAb\rightarrow\) \(1\text{s}\)    
      \(\) \(\PalAb{(10\times\text{ smaller})}\) \(\) \(\PalAb{(10\times\text{ smaller})}\)    

As we move left, each position is \(\PalAa{10}\) times bigger, and as we move right, each position is \(\PalAb{10}\) times smaller.

But what if we continue past hundreds as we move left and continue past units as we move right?  What is \(\PalAa{10\times}\) bigger than hundreds and what is \(\PalAb{10\times}\) smaller than units?

Thousands \(\PalAa\leftarrow\) Hundreds \(\PalAa\leftarrow\) Tens \(\PalAb\rightarrow\) Units \(\PalAb\rightarrow\) Tenths
\(1\Tsp000\text{s}\) \(\PalAa\leftarrow\) \(100\text{s}\) \(\PalAa\leftarrow\) \(10\text{s}\) \(\PalAb\rightarrow\) \(1\text{s}\) \(\PalAb\rightarrow\) \(\displaystyle\frac{1}{10}\text{s}\)
\(\PalAa{(10\times\text{ bigger})}\)   \(\PalAa{(10\times\text{ bigger})}\)       \(\PalAb{(10\times\text{ smaller})}\)   \(\PalAb{(10\times\text{ smaller})}\)

Thousands \((1\Tsp000\text{s})\) are \(\PalAa{10\times}\) bigger than hundreds and tenths \((\displaystyle\frac{1}{10}\text{s})\) are \(\PalAb{10\times}\) 
smaller than units.
