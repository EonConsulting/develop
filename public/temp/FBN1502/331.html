Present and future values

To understand the time value of money, we need to consider the present and future value concepts.  

If \( P\) is invested at an interest rate of \(i\) per period for a term of length \(\PalAb n\) periods, it accumulates to

\( \PalBc S=\PalAa P(1+i)^\PalAb n\)

and we call \(\PalAa P\) the present value and \(\PalBc S\) the future value of the investment.

Sometimes we are given the future value \(\PalBc S\) and wish to know the present value \(\PalAa P\).  To do this we can rearrange the above formula as follows:

\(\begin{align} \PalBc S&=\PalAa P(1+i)^\PalAb n\\ \PalAa P\times(1+i)^\PalAb n&=\PalBc S\\ \PalAa P &=\PalBc S\div(1+i)^\PalAb n\\ \PalAa P &=\PalBc S\times(1+i)^{-\PalAb n} \end{align}\)

In this latter case of finding the present value of some future value, the present value \(\PalAa {PV}\) is often referred to as the discounted value of the future sum and the factor \((1+i)^{-\PalAb n}\) is termed the discount factor.

This must not be confused with simple discounting for which a discount rate is defined.  The rate used in compound discounting is simply the relevant interest rate.  It would indeed be possible to introduce a compound discount rate, but this is not used in practice since it does not have a readily understood meaning.

The important concepts are those of present value and future value and the fact that the process of determining present value is often referred to as discounting.
