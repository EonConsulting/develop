Axes of the Cartesian plane

Compare the pictures below of the Cartesian plane as a map and the Cartesian plane as a grid.  Note the similarities and differences.

Cartesian plane as a map

Cartesian plane as a grid

In these two pictures several things are different.  Note the following differences:

  • In the grid we have put arrows at the end of all the lines.  This simply means that the grid goes on forever in all directions.
  • The compass directions (north, south, east and west) in the map are replaced with positive and negative numbers in the grid.  East and north are positive, while west and south are negative.
  • Middle Street on the map is now the \(\PalAa y\)-axis in the grid. The Cartesian plane has only one vertical axis, known as the \(y\)-axis.
  • Main Road on the map is now the  \(\PalAb x\)-axis in the grid.  The Cartesian plane has only one horizontal axis, known as the \(x\)-axis.
  • The Town Hall at the centre of town on the map is replaced by the word origin. (You need not write the word in when using the Cartesian plane.) It shows us that the middle point has a special name. This is the place you start from when finding your way through the Cartesian plane. The origin is often labelled \(0\).

At the origin the \(x\)-axis and \(y\)-axis perpendicularly
intersect at their respective zero points.
