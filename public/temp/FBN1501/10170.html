GDP omits

By now we know that the gross domestic product or GDP measures the total market value of all final goods and services produced within a country in one year.

Economists believe that the GDP is not the best measure for a country's progress of well-being.  The following internet site lists a few reasons why they feel the GDP must be replaced by a better measure:
http://staffwww.fullcoll.edu/fchan/macro/2gdp_definition.htm

Intermediate goods (goods that are inputs in the production of other goods) are not included in GDP to avoid double counting.  In another words, only the value added is counted.

GDP only counts the value of final products produced within a geographical boundary of a country.  If South African citizens are working in Europe, they do not contribute to the South African GDP, but Europe's GDP.

GDP sums the rand value of what has been produced in the economy over the year, not what was actually sold.  For example, used car sales are not included.

GDP was not designed to be a measure of well being of the society.  GDP omits the following:

  1. Non-marketable goods and services: tasks that do not involve market transactions, such as baby sitting, house cleaning, lawn mowing and so forth.
    Some very useful output is excluded because it is unpaid employment.
  2. Underground activities: illegal or cash transactions have no record.
    Government's estimates on these transactions are not accurate.
  3. Sales of used items: GDP measures only current output.  Used car and thrift stores' transactions are not counted.
  4. Financial transactions: trading existing assets, such as stock or bond purchases.
  5. Transfer payments: either government or private transfer payments are not included because goods and services are not produced in this process.  Examples are social security benefits or kid's allowances.
  6. Leisure: individuals consume leisure, just as they consume all the other tangible goods, which generate satisfactions.  But leisure has no price tag, so it is not included in GDP.
  7. Social costs: production processes may cause pollution.  Polluted air and water may have social costs like bad air quality, and cancer patients.  These social costs reduce our economic well-being.  If money is not spent to clean up the oil spill or to cure the cancer patients, those expenses are not added to the GDP.
  8. GDP does not measure quality or nature of the product, which contributes to the satisfaction level of the consumers.  Nominal GDP simply adds the dollar value of the product; it makes no differences if the product is a weapon, or a book.
