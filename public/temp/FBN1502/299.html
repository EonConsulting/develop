Farmer example recap

A peanut farmer’s cost function in rand, is given by

\(\PalAa C(\PalAb x)=\displaystyle\frac{\PalAb x^2}{100}+100\)

where \(\PalAb x\) represents the number of kilograms of peanuts produced.

The slope of this cost function is not constant and there is no constant rate of change. The slope changes continuously.

If the farmer produces \({400}\) kilograms of peanuts, how much will it cost to produce one extra kilogram?  This means, he wants to determine the marginal cost at a production level of \({400}\) kilograms.

Calculate the cost and the marginal cost for \(\PalAb{400}\) kilograms of peanuts:

The cost function is

\(\PalAa C(\PalAb x)\)

\(=\)

\(\displaystyle\frac{\PalAb x^2}{100}+100\)

Cost for \(\PalAb{400}\) kilograms

\(\PalAa C(\PalAb {400})\)

\(=\)

\(\displaystyle\frac{\PalAb {400}^2}{100}+100\)
   

\(=\)

\(\PalAa{1\Tsp700}\)

The derivative is

\(\PalBb {C'}(\PalAb x)\)

\(=\)

\(\displaystyle\frac{\PalAb x}{50}\)

Marginal cost for \(\PalAb{400}\) kilograms

\(\PalBb {C'}(\PalAb {400})\)

\(=\)

\(\displaystyle\frac{\PalAb {400}}{50}\)
   

\(=\)

\(\PalBb8\)

If the farmer produces \({400}\) kilograms, then it will cost him an extra \({\Cur8\Cdp00}\) to produce the next kilogram.

We know this because \(\PalBb {C'}(\PalAb {400})=\PalBb8\) means that at a production level of  \(\PalAb{400}\) kilograms, the marginal cost function approximates that it will cost the farmer an extra \(\PalBb{\Cur8\Cdp00}\) to produce the next kilogram of peanuts.
