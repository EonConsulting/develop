Real vs nominal

The consumer price index is used in many applications.  One of its most important implications is that of inflation adjustment (also known as deflation).  The CPI can be used to adjust home prices, wages, salaries, pensions or any other amount to reveal their real value.  

If you tell me your yearly salary is \(\Cur 200 \Tsp 000 \Cdp00\), you are referring to a current rand or nominal value.

Nominal or current values are the amounts in rand received or paid.

Over time these nominal values lose comparability unless they are adjusted for inflation.

Real values or inflation adjusted values are nominal values adjusted
for changes in the CPI.

Example

Suppose your nominal income this year is \(\PalAb{\Cur 200 \Tsp 000\Cdp00 }\).  How does its purchasing power compare with your \(\PalAb{\Cur 100 \Tsp 000 \Cdp00}\) income of ten years ago?  
Are you better off or worse off?

If all prices also doubled, it would take twice as much money now to buy the goods you bought ten years ago and you would be no better off.

How do I convert these nominal rand values of \(\PalAb{100\Tsp000}\) and \(\PalAb{200\Tsp000}\) to real rand values?

The answer is: by deflating.

Deflating is the process of ensuring the comparability of nominal
values by adjusting them for inflation.  This results in real values
which can be compared.

The general formula for deflating any variable is:

\(\PalAa{\text{real value}}=\displaystyle\frac{\PalAb{\text{nominal value in rands}}}{\text{CPI}}\times100 \)
