Rebasing

Rebasing of the consumer price index (CPI) refers to the process of resetting all of the actual indexes to a common starting point in a particular period, for example \(100\).
This is a bit like restarting a race, because by bringing all indexes to a common point, the historical divergences between indexes of different products are eliminated.
Failing to do this would inflate the effect of high-inflation items on the overall index and understate the effect of low-inflation items.

The CPI index is calculated from a base year which carries a value of \(100\).  All previous and subsequent periods are compared against the base year prices and expressed as a number in relation to the base index year value of \(100\).  

Prices of products and services generally increase over time and periods before the base year, therefore these periods generally have values that are less than \(100\), while periods after the base year generally have values that are greater than \(100\).  

The second last rebasing of the CPI index occurred in January 2009 and was calculated based on the prices for products and services in 2008.  These prices are therefore used as the base prices of the index - the average index value for the entire 2008 year was represented by an index value of \(\PalAb{100}\).  The CPI of South Africa that was measured after 2008 is less than \(100\), and not more than \(100\) as expected.  This can be due to the fact that changes were made to the CPI in 2009 to bring it in line with international standards.  

Traditionally, the South African CPI used the average of a year as the base period.
However, using a single month instead makes the linking in of the new weights and products much smoother than it would be using the yearly average.  This single month method was used in December 2012.  

The latest CPI weights were introduced in 2013 and are derived primarily from Statistics SA's household expenditure survey.  The weights were price-updated to December 2012 (known as the weights reference period with an index value of \(\PalAa{100}\)).
In this case the prices of products and services generally increased over time.
So periods before December 2012 therefore generally have values that are less than \(100\), while periods after December 2012 generally have values that are greater than \(100\).  

International practice is to rebase the CPI periodically to avoid distortions that build up over time.
