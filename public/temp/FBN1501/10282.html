Three dimensions

We can describe length as being one dimensional and area as two dimensional.  But for an object to take up space in the real world, it needs a third dimension.  Volume is a way of measuring three dimensional spaces.

The standard way to measure liquid is as volume.  Volume can be in litres.

Questions such as "How much water is in that cup, that aquarium, or that pool?" are examples of volume problems.  Volume is the space an item or substance occupies.

We generally measure liquids in litres \((\ell)\) or millilitres \((\text{m}\ell)\).  And for solids we generally measure volume in cubic metres \((\text{m}^3)\) or cubic centimetres \((\text{cm}^3)\).

The SI unit for volume is the cubic metre or \(\text{m}^3\).

The volume of a cube where each side is one metre in length is one cubic metre.  A cube with a volume of \(\PalAa{1\:\text{m}^3}\) can hold a thousand litres.
