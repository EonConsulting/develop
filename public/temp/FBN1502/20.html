Linear functions are used in many real-world situations. Graphing the functions can give an unbelievable amount of information and help you to solve problems more easily.

The equation of a linear function

An equation is defined as linear when its graph presents a linear function, in other words, a straight line. The equation of a linear function is 

\(y=f(x)=ax+b\)

where \(a\) and \(b\) are constants.

The intercepts of a straight line

Let us look at the general properties of a straight line. We are going to talk about \(x\)- and \(y\)-intercepts.

The \(\PalAb x\)-intercept of a straight line is the point where it crosses the \(\PalAb x\)-axis.
The \(\PalAa y\)-intercept of a straight line is the point where it crosses the \(\PalAa y\)-axis.

Example

The \(\PalAb x\)-intercept is the point \(\PalAb {(2; 0)}\) This is where \(\PalAb {y=0}\).  Sometimes the \(\PalAb x\)-intercept is defined as the \(\PalAb x\)-coordinate of the point where the line crosses the \(\PalAb x\)-axis.  

The \(\PalAa y\)-intercept is the point \(\PalAa {(0; 4)}\).  This is where \(\PalAa {x = 0}\).  Sometimes the \(\PalAa y\)-intercept is defined as the \(\PalAa y\)-coordinate of the point where the line crosses the \(\PalAa y\)-axis.
