Formula

If we have \(\PalAa n\) values in a data set and they have values \(x_1, x_2,\ldots,x_\PalAa n\), the sample mean, usually denoted by \(\bar{x}\) (pronounced "\(x\) bar"), is

\(\bar{x} = \displaystyle\frac{\left(x_1+x_2+\ldots+x_\PalAa n\right)}{\PalAa n}\)

The formula is usually written in a different way:

\(\bar{x} = \displaystyle\frac{\displaystyle\sum_{\PalBc i=1}^{\PalAa n} x_\PalBc i}{\PalAa n}\)

where \(\bar{x}\) (read as "\(x\) bar") is the generally accepted symbol for the arithmetic mean,
  \(\PalAa n\) is the number of observations,
  \(\sum\) (pronounced "sigma") is the Greek letter for S and means "sum of", and
  \(x_\PalBc i\) represents the \(\PalBc{ i^{\text{th}}}\) observation.

The above formula refers to the sample mean.  So, why have we called it a sample mean?  This is because, in statistics, samples and populations have very different meanings, which are very important, even if they are calculated in the same way in the case of the mean.  We will only consider the sample mean.

You will notice that the mean is not often one of the actual values that you observe in your data set.  However, one of its important properties is that it minimises error in the prediction of any one value in your data set.
