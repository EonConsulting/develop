An introduction to simple and compound interest

What is the difference between simple interest and compound interest?

Simple interest is interest paid on the original principal only.

Suppose Paul invests \(\Cur 600 \Cdp 00\) for three years at a simple interest rate of \(10\%\) per annum. Determine the simple interest that he earns.

Interest formula

\(I=Pin\)

Substitute the variables

\(I=\Cur600\Cdp00\times0 \Ndp 10\times3 \)

Calculate

\(I=\Cur180\Cdp00\)

At the end he will have earned \(\Cur 180 \Cdp 00 \) in interest. 

The accrued principal (accumulated sum or future value) will be calculated as:

Accrued principal

\(=P+I\)

Substitute the variables

\(=\Cur600\Cdp00+\Cur180\Cdp00\)
Calculate

\(=\Cur780\Cdp00\)

The accrued principal (accumulated sum or future value) is \(\Cur 780 \Cdp 00\).  So actually, every year the bank started with \(\Cur 600 \Cdp 00 \) to calculate the interest.

Compound interest is the interest earned on the original
principal, as well as on all interests earned previously.

In other words, with compound interest, at the end of each year, the interest earned is added to the original amount and the money is reinvested.
