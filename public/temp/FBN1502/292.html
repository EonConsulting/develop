Marginal cost, linear relationship and slope

Change consists of two components, namely size and direction.

Consider the example of a factory that manufactures office desks.  The manufacturing costs for a month are:

  cost of wood and other materials amount to \(\Cur105\Cdp00\) per desk,
  direct labour costs \(\Cur175\Cdp00\) per desk,
  variable factory overheads amount to \(\Cur70\Cdp00\) per desk and
  fixed factory overheads amount to \(\Cur100\Tsp000\Cdp00\) for the month.

The total cost is the linear function \(\PalAa{TC}=100\Tsp000+350\PalAb q\) of the number of desks \((\PalAb q)\) manufactured.  It gives a constant marginal cost of \(\Cur350\Cdp00\) per desk manufactured.

Consider the graph of this linear equation \(\PalAa{TC}=100\Tsp000+350\PalAb q\):

The slope is the change in total cost \((\PalAa{TC})\) which corresponds to change of one unit in the value of the quantity \((\PalAb q)\).

\(\begin{align} \PalBb{\text{slope}}&= \displaystyle\frac{\PalAa{\text{change in }TC}}{\PalAb{\text{change in }q}} \\ &=\displaystyle\frac{350}{1} \\ &=350 \end{align}\)

The value of the slope gives the size of the change.  The sign of the slope gives the direction of the change.

In this case the slope is positive, indicating an increase.  If the slope has a negative sign it indicates a decrease.
