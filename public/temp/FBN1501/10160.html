Method 2

It represents the quarterly growth at a seasonally adjusted and
annualised rate.  It reflects the changes in the real GDP from
one quarter to the next.
  • It is calculated by raising the percentage change between the two quarters to the power of \(\PalBc4\).
  • The quarters are seasonally adjusted to remove all seasonal effects before its annualised.
  • It is assumed that the percentage change from the one quarter to the following quarter will be maintained for the entire year.
  • It is called annualising and is used in South Africa as the official economic growth rate.
  • The advantage is that the quarterly growth rates can directly be compared with previous annual movements.
  • The disadvantage is that sometimes the annualised data can be very volatile due to the effects of any irregular occurrences during a specific quarter, for example, labour disputes.

The formula used to calculate the percentage change between two quarters at an annualized rate is given below:

\(\text{(Q/Q) growth rate}= \left( \left( \displaystyle\frac{\PalAa{\text{GDP}}_{\PalAa{\text{current quarter}}}}{\PalAb{\text{GDP}}_{\PalAb{\text{previous quarter}}}}\right)^\PalBc4-1 \right) \times 100\)

Annualising is to express the percentage change in the GDP between two consecutive quarters of a year in yearly terms, even though the percentage change does not directly apply to a year.

The percentage change in GDP between two consecutive quarters has been mathematically converted to yearly terms in an annualised number.

The annualised percentage change in the GDP is often theoretical because there is no guarantee that the percentage change in the GDP will be maintained between the remaining quarters of the entire year.
