Definition of a reciprocal

When the product of two fractions equals one, the fractions are reciprocals.

Every non-zero fraction has a reciprocal.  It is easy to determine the reciprocal of a fraction, because all you have to do is switch the numerator and denominator.  That means, just turn the fraction around.

Examples

To find the reciprocal of \(\displaystyle\frac{3}{4}\), just switch the numerator and denominator around.  So the reciprocal of \(\displaystyle\frac{3}{4}\) is \(\displaystyle\frac{4}{3}\).  We know \(\PalAa{\displaystyle\frac{3}{4}}\) and \(\PalAb{\displaystyle\frac{4}{3}}\) are reciprocals because:

\(\displaystyle\PalAa{\displaystyle\frac{3}{4}}\times\PalAb{\displaystyle\frac{4}{3}}=\displaystyle\frac{12}{12}=1\)

To find the reciprocal of a whole number, just put one over the whole number.  For example, the reciprocal of \(2\) is \(\displaystyle\frac{1}{2}\).  We know \(\PalAa2\) and \(\PalAb{\displaystyle\frac{1}{2}}\) are reciprocals because:

\(\displaystyle\PalAa{\displaystyle\frac{2}{1}}\times\PalAb{\displaystyle\frac{1}{2}}=\displaystyle\frac{2}{2}=1\)
