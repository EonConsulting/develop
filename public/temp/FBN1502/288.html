Introduction

The concept of marginal cost is very important in the theory of economics.
Marginal cost is the change in total cost that comes from making or producing one additional item.

Marginal cost is defined as the cost that results
from a one unit increase in the production rate.

So if a company has already produced \(13\) cars, the marginal cost of the \(14^{\text{th}}\) car is only the additional costs the company incurs in making that car.

Expanding scale of production

A company can have cost advantages by expanding their scale of production.

The cost of producing something can fall as the volume of its output increases.  Hence it might cost \(\Cur3\Tsp000\Cdp00\) to produce \(100\) copies of a magazine, but only \(\Cur4\Tsp000\Cdp00\) to produce \(1\Tsp000\) copies.  The cost in this case has fallen from \(\Cur30\Cdp00\) to \(\Cur4\Cdp00\) a copy, because the main elements of cost in producing a magazine, that is editorial and design, are unrelated to the number of magazines produced.

The aim is to reduce the production cost per unit as production increases.
In other words:

The cost of producing an additional unit, the marginal
cost, decreases as the volume of production increases.

Optimum production level

The purpose of analysing marginal cost is to determine at what point a company can operate at an optimum production level.

Companies constantly examine the cost implication of adding one more unit to their production schedules.  This is because at some point, the benefit of producing one additional unit and generating revenue from that item will bring the overall cost of producing the product line down.

The key to optimizing manufacturing costs is to
find this point or level as quickly as possible.
