Summary of laws

Communicative laws:    \(\begin{align}\PalAa A+\PalAb B&=\PalAb B+\PalAa A\\\PalAa A\times\PalAb B&=\PalAb B\times\PalAa A\end{align}\)                   

Associative laws:          \(\begin{align}(\PalAa A+\PalAb B)+\PalBc C&=\PalAa A+(\PalAb B+\PalBc C)\\(\PalAa A\times\PalAb B)\times\PalBc C&=\PalAa A\times(\PalAb B\times\PalBc C)\end{align}\)  

Distributive law:          \(\PalAa A \times (\PalAb B+\PalBc C)=\PalAa A\times \PalAb B+\PalAa A\times\PalBc C\)

But be careful!

Commutative laws not for subtraction and division

The commutative laws do not work for subtraction and division, for example:

\(\begin{align}\PalAa 7-\PalAb 3&\neq\PalAb 3-\PalAa 7\\\PalAa {10}\div\PalAb 2&\neq\PalAb2\div\PalAa {10}\end{align}\)

The equals sign with a line through is read as "does not equal".

In the subtraction calculation, commutation (changing the order) leads to the negative of the initial result, that is \(-4\) instead of \(4\):

\(\PalAa 7-\PalAb 3=4\)  while  \(\PalAb 3-\PalAa 7=-4\)

In the division calculation, commutation leads to the inverse of the initial result, that is \(\frac{1}{5}\) instead of \(5\):

\(\PalAa {10}\div\PalAb 2=5\)  while  \(\PalAb2\div\PalAa {10}=\displaystyle\frac{1}{5}\)

But we can transform expressions containing subtraction and division to ones containing only addition and multiplication.

To be specific, in the case of subtraction we change the sign of the number being subtracted and then add, for example:

\(\PalAa7-\PalAb3=\PalAa7+\PalAb{-3}=\PalAb{-3}+\PalAa7=4\)

In the case of division we can replace the number being divided by with its inverse and multiply, for example:

\(\PalAa{10}\div\PalAb2=\PalAa{10}\times\PalAb{2^{-1}}=\PalAb{2^{-1}}\times\PalAa{10}=5\)

Associative laws not for subtraction and division

The associative laws do not work for subtraction and division, for example:

\(\begin{align}\PalAa {10}-(\PalAb 5-\PalBc 2)&\neq(\PalAa {10}-\PalAb 5)-\PalBc2\\(\PalAa {8}\div\PalAb 4)\div\PalBc2&\neq\PalAa8\div(\PalAb {4}\div\PalBc2)\end{align}\)

We know that when you have more than one operation with the same priority you just operate from left to right.  This means that an expression like \(8\div4\div2\) is highly ambiguous, because the result depends on the order in which the operations are performed.  If you want a specific order you must enforce that using brackets.

The conclusion is that, whereas for addition and multiplication we can change the order of the factors, for subtraction and division we cannot.  Or rather, if we do, we must do so with care.
