Fractional exponents 1

Another way to write roots is to write them as fractional exponents.

A fractional exponent is a way to write a root without the root sign.  Then the expression is evaluated using the exponent rules.  For example, the fourth root is denoted by an exponent of \(\frac{1}{4}\) and the fifth root is denoted by an exponent of \(\frac{1}{5}\).
Every root is denoted by an exponent with \(1\) in the numerator and the order of root in the denominator.

A fractional exponent like \(\PalAb{\frac{1}{n}}\) means to take the \(\PalAb{n^{\text{th}}}\) root:

\(\sqrt[\PalAb n]{x}\)

\(=\)

\(x^{\PalAb{\frac{1}{n}}}\)

A number to the power of a half is the square root:

\(\sqrt{x}\)

\(=\)

\(x^{\PalAb{\frac{1}{2}}}\)

A number to the power of a third is the cube root:

\(\sqrt[\PalAb 3]{x}\)

\(=\)

\(x^{\PalAb{\frac{1}{3}}}\)

A number to the power of a seventh is the seventh root:

 \(\sqrt[\PalAb 7]{x}\) \(=\) \(x^{\PalAb{\frac{1}{7}}}\)

Examples

Write the following two roots as fractional exponents:

\(\sqrt[3]{x^9y^6}\)   and   \(\sqrt[4]{x^4y^8}\)

Writing these roots as fractional exponents will give:

 

\(\sqrt[\PalAb 3]{x^9y^6}\)

\(=\)

\(\left(x^9y^6 \right)^{\PalAb{\frac{1}{3}}}\)
   

\(=\)

\(x^{\frac{9}{1}\times\PalAb{\frac{1}{3}}}y^{\frac{6}{1}\times\PalAb{\frac{1}{3}}}\)
   

\(=\)

\(x^3y^2\)
       

and

\(\sqrt[\PalAb4]{x^4y^8}\)

\(=\)

\(\left(x^4y^8 \right)^{\PalAb{\frac{1}{4}}}\)
   

\(=\)

\(x^{\frac{4}{1}\times\PalAb{\frac{1}{4}}}y^{\frac{8}{1}\times\PalAb{\frac{1}{4}}}\)
   

\(=\)

\(xy^2\)
