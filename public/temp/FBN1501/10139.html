Example

Brackets are used to override preconceived priorities.  Brackets are a very powerful means of casting expressions in a form that conveys the specific meaning that we want.

Any expression between brackets is regarded as a number which must be determined first before proceeding further with the calculation.  Any expression?  Yes.

But an expression itself may contain brackets.  Does this mean that we can have brackets within brackets?  Yes, indeed it does.  That is, in fact, the beauty and the power of brackets.

Simplify the following expression:

\(\sqrt{(3\times\sqrt{(4^2+3^2)}+(4+3)^2)}\)

Solution

Step 1

 

\(\sqrt{(3\times\sqrt{(\PalAa{4^2+3^2})}+(\PalAb{4+3})^2)}\)

Calculate the two sets of innermost brackets first, it does not matter which one first.

Step 2

\(=\)

\(\sqrt{(3\times\sqrt{(\PalAa{16+9})}+\PalAb{7}^2)}\)

Within a set of brackets the usual priority rules apply: in the first set, addition after exponentiation.

Step 3

\(=\)

\(\sqrt{(3\times\sqrt{\PalAa{25}}+\PalAb{7}^2)}\)

Next do the exponentiation \(\left( \sqrt{25}=25^{\frac{1}{2}} \right)\) and \(7^2\).

Step 4

\(=\)

\(\sqrt{(3\times5+49)}\)

Multiplication \((3\times5)\) is before addition.

Step 5

\(=\)

\(\sqrt{(15+49)}\)

Addition.

Step 6

\(=\)

\(\sqrt{64}\)

Now we can take the square root.

Step 7

\(=\)

\(8\)

Result of simplification.

So, the simplification of the mathematical expression results in \(8\).
