Summary

We can recognise, even if only intuitively, that the potential uses for the quadratic function are considerable.

It is evident that they can be used to model situations in which the dependent variable is expected to peak, or pass through a dip, for some value of the independent variable, for example in demand, supply and profit functions.

  • The general equation for a parabola or quadratic function is \(y=ax^2+bx+c\) where \(a\), \(b\) and \(c\) are constants and \(a\neq0\).
     
  • If \(a>0\) the parabola opens upwards (smiling face) and
    if \(a<0\) the parabola opens downwards (sad face).
     
  • Changes in the value of \(b\) affects the placement of the parabola in a horizontal shift (along the \(x\)-axis).
     
  • Changes in the value of \(c\) affects the placement of the parabola in a vertical shift (along the \(y\)-axis).
     
  • The coordinates of the vertex of the parabola are \(\left( \displaystyle\frac{-b}{2a};f\left(\displaystyle\frac{-b}{2a}\right) \right)\).
     
  • The \(x\)-intercepts (if any) are found by solving \(f(x)=0\) with

\(x=\displaystyle\frac{-b-\sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a}\) and \(x=\displaystyle\frac{-b+\sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a}\).
 

  • The \(y\)-coordinate of the \(y\)-intercept is â€‹\(y=f(0)=c\), 

so the \(y\)-intercept has coordinates \((0;c)\).
 

  • The discriminant \(d\) is calculated as \(b^2-4ac\)

Specific cases of the discriminant:

\(d>0\)

Two separate \(x\)-intercepts exist.
The intercepts are equally far from \(x\)-coordinate of vertex.

\(d=0\)

There is only one \(x\)-intercept and it is also the vertex.
The parabola just touches the \(x\)-axis at the vertex.

\(d<0\)

There are no intercepts on the \(x\)-axis.
The parabola also does not touch the \(x\)-axis.
  • The slope of a parabola is ever-changing in contrast to a straight line which has a constant slope.
