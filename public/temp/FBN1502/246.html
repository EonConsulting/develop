In general

Regarding exponents, any number raised to the power of one, is equal to the number and any number raised to the power of zero, is equal to one.

Therefore, for logarithms there are two general results in particular:

Because \(a\PalAa{^1}=\PalAa a\), it follows that \(\log_a\PalAb a=\PalAb1\),
and
because \(a^\PalAa0=\PalAa1\), it follows that \(\log_a\PalAb1=\PalAb0\).
  • Note that the base in the two cases above is equal to \(a\).
  • When the base is \(10\) you get the common logarithm \(\log_{10}y\), which is sometimes just written as \(\log y\).
  • When the base is \(e\), you get the natural logarithm.  The calculator has a key for \(\log_e\) which is usually denoted by the symbol \(ln\).  (The symbol \(e\) is a famous number in mathematics.  It is called Euler's number and approximates to \(2\Ndp718\).)
  • In computer science we have \(lg\) which is the logarithm base \(2\), or \(\log_2\).
