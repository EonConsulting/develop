Introduction

René Descartes was a French man who lived in the 1600s.  He invented the rectangular coordinate system or plane.  In fact, this coordinate system is sometimes called the Cartesian plane in his honour.  Using this plane, we can describe any point in the plane using an ordered pair of numbers.

The Cartesian plane is like a map grid.  You can locate any point in the Cartesian plane if you know two things:

  • how far the point is to the left or right, and
  • how far the point is up or down.

Let us start by looking at a map, given below.  In this city map, the Town Hall is right at the centre of the town.  As you travel east of the centre of town, the streets are named 10th Street East, 11th Street East and so forth.  You find similar patterns as you drive west, north or south.  Middle Street is really zero east and zero west simultaneously, so it is easiest to just give it a name.  Similarly, Main Road is simultaneously zero north and zero south.

You can easily find any location on the map just by knowing how far it is east or west and north or south of the centre of town. The car is at about the crossing of 11th Street East and 11th Street North.

For the Cartesian plane we use positive and negative numbers.  If Middle Street is at zero east and west, and Main Road is at zero north and south, then east and north will be positive while west and south will be negative.
