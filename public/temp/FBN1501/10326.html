Example

The mean is the measure of locality used most often.  However, sometimes it can be misleading.

Consider the monthly salaries (in \(\Cur1\Tsp000\Cdp00\)) of ten staff members at a factory, given below:

  Staff   \(\PalAa1\) \(\PalAa2\) \(\PalAa3\) \(\PalAa4\) \(\PalAa5\) \(\PalAa6\) \(\PalAa7\) \(\PalAa8\) \(\PalAa9\) \(\PalAa{10}\)
  Salary   \(15\) \(18\) \(16\) \(14\) \(15\) \(15\) \(12\) \(17\) \(90\) \(95\)

The mean is:

\(\begin{align} \bar x&=\displaystyle\frac{\left(15+18+16+14+15+15+12+17+90+95\right)}{\PalAa{10}}\\ &=30\Ndp70 \end{align}\)

The mean salary for these ten staff members is therefore:

 \(\Cur1\Tsp000\Cdp00\times30\Ndp70=\Cur30\Tsp700\Cdp00\).  

However, inspecting the data suggests that this mean value of \(\Cur30\Tsp700\Cdp00\) 
might not be the best way to accurately reflect the typical salary of a staff member, since most staff members have salaries in the \(\Cur12\Tsp000\Cdp00\) to \(\Cur18\Tsp000\Cdp00\) range.

The mean is rather sensitive to outliers and may often be misleading.  
Outliers are values that are unusual compared to the rest of the data set by being especially small or large in numerical value.  On its own, without any additional information, the arithmetic mean may often lead to incorrect conclusions.  In this situation, the mean is being skewed by the two large salaries.  Therefore, we would like to have a better measure of central tendency.  Taking the median would be a better measure of central tendency in this situation.

A big advantage of the arithmetic mean is that it uses all the available data.  This is not the case for the other measures of locality.

Since the mean can be calculated exactly, it forms the basis for many advanced analyses and is not only descriptive in nature.
