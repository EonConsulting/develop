In practice

Perimeter

The perimeter is the length of the outline of a shape.

To find the perimeter of a rectangle or square you have to add the lengths of all the four sides.  In this case, illustrated below,  \(l\) is the length of the rectangle, while \(w\) is the width.

The perimeter \(P\) is:

\(\begin{align} P&=l+l+w+w\\ &=2l+2w \\ &=2(l+w) \end{align}\)

Scale drawings

A map cannot be of the same size as the area it represents.  So, the measurements are scaled down to make the map of a size that can be conveniently used by users such as motorists, cyclists and bushwalkers.  A scale drawing of a building (or bridge) has the same shape as the real building (or bridge) that it represents, but a different size.
Builders use scaled drawings to make buildings and bridges.

A ratio is used in scale drawings of maps and buildings.  The ratios are:

\(\begin{align} \text{scale of drawing}&=\PalAa{\text{drawing length}}:\PalAb{\text{actual length}}\\ \text{map scale}&=\PalAa{\text{map distance}}:\PalAb{\text{actual distance}} \end{align}\)

A scale is usually expressed in one of two ways:

  • using units as in \(1\text{cm}\) to \(1\text{km}\)
  • without explicitly mentioning units as in \(1:100\Tsp000\)

A scale of \(\PalAa1:\PalAb{100\Tsp000}\) means that the real distance is \(\PalAb{100\Tsp000}\) times the length of \(\PalAa1\) unit on the map or drawing.
