Random selection

A political candidate wants to determine the opinions of the voters in his ward.
He decides on a sample of size \(20\).  Using random numbers he chooses \(20\) telephone numbers from the telephone directory for a telephonic survey.  Is this procedure correct to find a random sample?  Why not?

Not all residents may have telephones and all the numbers of those who do have telephones may not be included in the telephone directory.  Therefore such a sample cannot be considered to be random.

Randomly choosing the members of a sample is important because it helps prevent bias in your results. Random selection allows impersonal choice to choose the sample, rather than the individual performing the poll (the sampler) to choose his/her own participants, or self-selection of respondents as in a voluntary response poll.  

If a sampler does not use random selection, the sample will favour the selection of certain groups, possibly without the sampler even realising it.

Difficulties and cautions 

In the real world, a truly random sample is difficult to achieve, but you can come close.  One of the most difficult steps is obtaining a complete list of every member in the population from which you want a sample.

A telephone book for a city is often used for sampling.  Some of the problems associated with using a telephone book are that it excludes those who do not have a telephone, those who have unlisted numbers, and more recently relevant, those who use mobile phones instead of land lines.

A second barrier to purely random samples is that it may be difficult or impossible to locate some people in the population.  For example, people who work unusual hours or who travel a lot may be chosen to be included in the sample, but are not available when you attempt to contact them.
