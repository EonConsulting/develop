The relationship between \(P\) and \(S\): Specific cases

How do you distinguish between the present and future value of an annuity?

Future value

Calculating the future value of an annuity allows you to see what
the value will be of the sum of individual amounts in the future.

Suppose you want a lump sum of \(\Cur 1000 \Tsp 000 \Cdp 00 \) in \(50\) years, the future value calculation will tell you how much you need to save each month at a given interest rate.  In other words, \(\Cur 1000 \Tsp 000 \Cdp 00\) is the future value of your regular monthly investment.

Present value

As an annuity is a series of equal payments or receipts, the present value
of an annuity is the sum of the present values of the individual amounts.

If you can afford a mortgage repayment of a certain amount per month, calculating the present value of the repayments at a fixed rate of interest over a given number of years, will tell you the principal sum you can afford to borrow.

So, suppose you can afford a monthly payment of \(\Cur 3 \Tsp 000 \Cdp 00 \) on a loan to buy a car, taken out on 30 May 2012 for \(48\) months beginning on 30 June 2012 at an interest rate of \(14\%\) per annum compounded monthly.  If you now calculate the price of the car, this would be the principal sum you can afford to borrow.

The present value is also the amount of money you need to invest now
in order to withdraw a certain amount of money for a specified period
of time in the future.

Suppose you invested a certain amount on 1 January 2010 in such a way that it generated a periodic payment of \(\Cur 1 \Tsp 500 \Cdp 00\) at the end of each month of the calendar year 2010 at an interest rate of \(13 \Ndp 2\% \) per year, compounded monthly.  If you now calculate the original investment, this would be the amount you needed to invest.
